The Story

A boy who lost his father to the fog. A doctor who taught him to see. A ship that never stopped haunting him.

Told in flashback by an elderly Arthur Conan Doyle, the film opens with the 1872 discovery of the Marie Celeste, found adrift with her crew vanished. Young Arthur, raised in a struggling Edinburgh household, watches his father — an artist unraveling into addiction and mental illness — disappear one night after an encounter with shadowy, glowing‑eyed figures. At the University of Edinburgh, Arthur comes under the mentorship of Dr. Joseph Bell, whose uncanny powers of deduction plant the seed for a character Arthur will later invent: Sherlock Holmes.

Seeking escape, Arthur signs on as a ship’s surgeon aboard the Arctic whaler Hope, where he loses a mentor figure, faces a crewmate suspected of serial murder, and is repeatedly visited by apparitions of the Marie Celeste amid the ice. Back in London, as the real Jack the Ripper terrorizes Whitechapel, Arthur is unofficially drawn into the investigation — applying Bell’s method to real crime scenes, and coming dangerously close to the killer himself. Through it all, one question never resolves: is Arthur inheriting his father’s madness, or discovering the raw material of his own genius?

Why Producers Are Circling

I.

Grounded in Real History

Dr. Joseph Bell, the Mary Celeste, and Jack the Ripper are documented fact, not invention — built‑in authenticity and press hooks no fictional mystery can match.

II.

The Detective Never Ages

Sherlock Holmes remains one of the most adapted, most bankable characters in the English language. This is the one story no adaptation has ever told: how he was born.

III.

Elevated Genre, Financeable Scale

A character‑driven period piece with real scale — the Arctic, Whitechapel — at a right‑sized budget. A prestige title, not a tentpole gamble.
